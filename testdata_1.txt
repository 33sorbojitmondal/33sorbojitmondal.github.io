The American Civil War was a bloody conflict between the Union and the Confederacy, primarily caused by the contentious issue of slavery, clashing perspectives on states' rights versus federal power, and socioeconomic differences between the industrialized North and the agrarian South. Significant events leading to and during the war included Lincoln's election, the attack on Fort Sumter, the Emancipation Proclamation, and battles like Gettysburg. The Union's victory resulted in the abolition of slavery, the preservation of the United States, and the strengthening of the federal government, though the South faced immense devastation and a challenging Reconstruction period.

The root causes of the American Civil War were multifaceted, but the primary driving force was the divisive debate over the institution of slavery, with the Southern states vehemently defending it and the Northern states seeking to limit its expansion. Compounding this issue were fundamental disagreements over the balance of power between states' rights and federal authority, as well as the economic and social disparities between the industrializing North and the agricultural South. Pivotal events that sparked and shaped the war included Lincoln's election, the Confederate attack on Fort Sumter, the Emancipation Proclamation, and major battles like Gettysburg. The Union's eventual victory led to the abolition of slavery, the preservation of the United States as a single nation, and the strengthening of the federal government's power, but the South faced widespread devastation and a long, difficult period of Reconstruction.
